The Geometry of Less

Why principal component analysis rotates axes rather than drops columns — and what survives the compression.

Dataset Iris (UCI, 1936)
Features 4 continuous measurements
Components 2 retained (95.8% variance)
Format Conceptual · Geometric

The Correlation Problem

Four numbers describe each Iris flower: sepal length, sepal width, petal length, petal width. Four dimensions, 150 specimens. On paper, that is a modest dataset. In practice, the four measurements do not move independently — long petals reliably accompany wide petals, and large sepals trend together with petal size. The four variables carry maybe two dimensions of genuine information.

This is the situation PCA addresses — not "too many features" in the abstract, but specifically redundant information packed into correlated columns. The technique finds linear combinations of the original variables that capture the maximum variance in successively orthogonal directions. Those combinations are the principal components: new axes that replace the old ones.

The Iris dataset is used here not because flowers are interesting but because the structure is well-understood, the correlation pattern is clean, and the two-dimensional result can be verified against ground truth (species labels). It is a specimen for examining the technique, not the other way around.

Why Dropping Columns Fails

The obvious response to correlation is to remove redundant features. If petal length and petal width are correlated, pick one. This approach has two flaws.

First, the correlation is rarely perfect. Petal length and petal width share variance but not all of it. Dropping one discards the portion unique to that measurement. Second, the choice of which column to drop is arbitrary — there is no principled reason petal length should survive over petal width. You are not compressing information; you are discarding it by editorial decision.

PCA instead rotates the coordinate system. No observation is removed. No feature is discarded. The algorithm finds a new axis — a direction through the four-dimensional space — along which the data varies the most. That direction becomes PC1. It then finds the orthogonal direction of maximum remaining variance. That becomes PC2. The process continues until you have as many components as original features, at which point you have simply repackaged the same information in a rotated frame.

Compression happens when you stop early: keep only the first two components and you discard the remaining variance — but it is the least informative variance, by construction.

Standardization First

PCA maximizes variance. That sounds desirable until you notice that features with larger numerical scales will dominate variance by sheer magnitude — not because they carry more information. A feature measured in millimetres will swamp one measured in units, even if the unit-measured feature carries more discriminating signal.

The fix is to standardize: subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation, so every feature enters with unit variance. For Iris, the four measurements are already in comparable units (centimetres), but the rule holds generally. Skipping standardization is the most common silent mistake in PCA applications outside academic toy datasets.

Standardization · z-score transform

z_i = (x_i − μ_i) / σ_i

Applied column-wise: each feature centered and scaled to σ=1

Covariance matrix after standardization = correlation matrix

Once standardized, PCA operates on the covariance matrix of the transformed data. For Iris, the resulting covariance matrix is 4×4 — one entry per feature pair. The off-diagonal entries tell you how much two features move together. The algorithm then finds the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of that matrix.

Eigenvectors are the principal components: directions in feature space. Eigenvalues are the amounts of variance explained along each direction. The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue is PC1; the one with the second-largest is PC2, and so on. They are guaranteed to be orthogonal because covariance matrices are symmetric positive semi-definite.

What PC1 Actually Represents

Look at the loading table below. PC1 loads positively and heavily on petal length (+0.58), petal width (+0.56), and sepal length (+0.52). Sepal width loads negatively and weakly (−0.27). In plain language: PC1 is a size axis. Flowers that score high on PC1 are large in nearly every dimension. Flowers that score low are small.

PC2 tells a different story. It loads overwhelmingly on sepal width (+0.923) and almost nothing on petal measurements. This is the axis of sepal proportion — how wide a flower's sepals are relative to everything else. That is not a redundant dimension; it captures variance that PC1 does not.

This interpretability is not always this clean. Iris is unusually well-structured. In messier real-world data, components may span many features at middling loadings and resist clean narrative. The loading table is still informative — it tells you which original variables are pulled into each component — but the botanical metaphor may not survive.

The Scree Plot and the Elbow Decision

The scree plot in Figure 1B shows variance explained by each component in descending order. PC1 captures 73.0%, PC2 captures 22.9%, PC3 captures 3.7%, and PC4 captures 0.5%. The cumulative total for two components is 95.8%.

The "elbow" heuristic says: stop where the curve bends sharply. For Iris, that is unambiguous — after PC2, the remaining components explain almost nothing. In practice, elbow decisions are frequently contested. The scree may not bend cleanly. There is no universally correct threshold; 90%, 95%, and 99% variance retention are all defensible depending on downstream use.

Critical Point

Choosing how many components to keep is a modelling decision, not an algorithmic output. PCA tells you the variance distribution; it cannot tell you how much compression is acceptable for your application. A clustering task can often tolerate 85% retention. A reconstruction task may demand 99%. Name the criterion before fitting.

A related mistake is interpreting variance explained as predictive accuracy. The components that explain the most variance are not necessarily the ones most predictive of a label. In classification or regression, supervised dimensionality reduction — LDA, supervised autoencoders — sometimes outperforms PCA precisely because PCA is indifferent to the label. It compresses by variance, not by relevance.

Reading the Two-Dimensional View

When you project the 150 Iris observations onto PC1 and PC2, three clusters appear. Setosa separates cleanly from the other two species along PC1 — it is a much smaller flower. Versicolor and Virginica overlap somewhat along PC1 but show different PC2 distributions.

This is an important result to read carefully. The separation you see in the two-dimensional plot is real in the sense that it reflects genuine biological difference captured by these measurements. But the axis labels "PC1" and "PC2" carry no inherent meaning. The meaning is constructed retroactively by examining the loadings. Without the loading table, the scatter plot is a coloured cloud floating in unlabelled space.

Reading Loadings Correctly

A point near the positive end of PC1 has high values of petal length, petal width, and sepal length simultaneously. A point near the positive end of PC2 has an unusually wide sepal. The axes are not features; they are linear combinations of features. Annotating them with feature names is incorrect.

The correct annotation is a loading vector diagram (biplot), which overlays the original feature directions on the PC space. Without a biplot, readers cannot interpret position in the PC plot.

There is a persistent misreading of PCA scatter plots in portfolio work: treating cluster separation as evidence that the clusters are "real" or "stable." Cluster separation in PCA space says only that these observations differ along the directions of maximum variance. It does not validate the clusters. You need stability analysis — bootstrapping the projection, checking that species-level separation holds across subsamples — to say anything about robustness.

What the Compression Discards

Dropping PC3 and PC4 discards 4.2% of variance. For most purposes, that is acceptable. But what specifically is in that 4.2%? Without investigation, you cannot know. It might be noise — measurement error and rounding artefacts that genuinely should not influence downstream analysis. Or it might be a small but real biological signal: a subspecies distinction, an altitude effect, a systematic measurement shift between collectors.

The scree plot cannot answer this. Neither can the loading table. Answering it requires domain knowledge: checking whether any known biological factor is disproportionately encoded in low-variance components. This is why PCA is a tool for informed practitioners, not a mechanical data preprocessing step.

In applied settings, the answer is usually noise, and dropping low-variance components genuinely improves downstream model performance by removing dimensions that would otherwise overfit to measurement artefacts. But "usually" is not "always," and the responsible path is to run the downstream analysis with and without the dropped components and check whether the results change materially.

The Code Behind the Geometry

The mechanics in scikit-learn are brief. The conceptual weight is in interpreting the output, not in calling the function.

import numpy as np
from sklearn.datasets import load_iris
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA

iris = load_iris()
X, y = iris.data, iris.target

# Standardize: critical step - skip this and large-magnitude
# features dominate the variance decomposition
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X)

# Fit PCA: n_components=None returns all components
pca = PCA(n_components=None, random_state=42)
pca.fit(X_scaled)

# Variance explained by each component
var_ratio = pca.explained_variance_ratio_
# array([0.7296, 0.2285, 0.0367, 0.0052])

# Cumulative variance - where does the elbow fall?
cumvar = np.cumsum(var_ratio)
# array([0.7296, 0.9581, 0.9949, 1.0000])

# Component loadings - the rotation matrix
# Shape: (n_components, n_features)
loadings = pca.components_
# PC1: [ 0.521, -0.269,  0.580,  0.565]
# PC2: [ 0.377,  0.923, -0.020, -0.006]

# Project onto first two components
X_pca = pca.transform(X_scaled)[:, :2]
# X_pca shape: (150, 2) - ready for scatter or clustering

A note on signs: PCA eigenvectors have an arbitrary sign convention. PC1 might have all positive loadings in one run and all negative in another, depending on the implementation's internal choice. The direction is identical; only the sign flips. When comparing loadings across libraries or runs, check the relative signs rather than the absolute values.

Reconstruction and the Reversibility of PCA

Unlike feature selection, PCA is mathematically reversible. If you keep all components, you can perfectly reconstruct the original standardized data. If you keep two out of four, the reconstruction is approximate — the 4.2% variance you dropped is permanently lost from that reconstruction.

# Partial reconstruction from 2 components
pca_2 = PCA(n_components=2, random_state=42)
X_pca2 = pca_2.fit_transform(X_scaled)
X_reconstructed = pca_2.inverse_transform(X_pca2)
# X_reconstructed shape: (150, 4) - in standardized space

# Reconstruction error (mean squared)
mse = np.mean((X_scaled - X_reconstructed) ** 2)
# mse ≈ 0.042 - the discarded 4.2% variance, now as error

This reconstruction framing is useful for anomaly detection: fit PCA on normal data, then flag observations whose reconstruction error is unusually high as potential anomalies. The 4.2% dropped from Iris generates essentially random reconstruction error — which is what noise looks like. A real anomaly generates structured reconstruction error in specific features.

When PCA Is the Wrong Tool

PCA is linear. It finds linear combinations of features. If the true low-dimensional structure is nonlinear — a spiral, a Swiss roll, a manifold with curvature — PCA will fail to recover it. In those cases, nonlinear dimensionality reduction (t-SNE, UMAP, Isomap) handles the geometry better, though at the cost of the mathematical guarantees and interpretability that PCA provides.

PCA is also unsupervised. It ignores the label entirely. For classification problems, this means the most variance-explaining components may not be the most class-discriminating. Linear Discriminant Analysis is the supervised counterpart: it finds directions that maximize between-class variance relative to within-class variance, which is a fundamentally different objective.

Finally, PCA assumes continuous, roughly normal features. Categorical features, count data, and heavy-tailed distributions all violate this assumption to varying degrees. Sparse count matrices (document-term, user-item) are better handled by truncated SVD (LSA) or NMF. Binary data is better handled by factor analysis with appropriate link functions.

Summary Constraint

PCA is the right tool when: features are continuous, correlation is the primary source of redundancy, interpretable components matter, and the downstream task tolerates a linear approximation. When any of these conditions fail, consider the alternatives before defaulting to PCA because it is familiar.

What the Iris View Reveals and Conceals

The two-component Iris projection reveals that botanical species differ substantially in overall size (PC1) and sepal proportion (PC2). Setosa is unambiguously distinct. Versicolor and Virginica are separable but not cleanly — their overlap in the PC space corresponds to the real-world difficulty of distinguishing medium specimens of the two species from measurement alone.

What the projection conceals is everything carried in PC3 and PC4. It also conceals the nonlinear structure that might exist within each species cluster. The scatter plot looks clean, but clean projections are a property of PCA's objective function, not of nature. PCA is maximally honest about variance along the kept axes and maximally uninformative about everything it dropped.

The appropriate conclusion from this analysis is not "PCA perfectly separates Iris species" — a naïve classifier on the raw features does that more reliably. The conclusion is that sepal and petal measurements are highly correlated, that two directions capture nearly all of their joint variation, and that those directions carry interpretable botanical meaning. That is a structural insight about the data, not a classification result.

PCA is worth understanding at the geometric level rather than the function-call level. The rotation is the algorithm. The rest — standardization, eigendecomposition, variance thresholds, loading interpretation — is the discipline of applying the rotation without misreading its outputs. The Iris dataset is a good specimen precisely because its structure is well-understood enough that you can check your interpretation against ground truth. The habit of checking interpretations against external ground truth is the one PCA skill that transfers to every other dataset.
